- first proposed by James Duessenberry
- Consumption of an individual is not the function of his absolute income but of his relative position in income in the society

### Four important components

1. Individuals are more concerned about relative well-being than absolute well-being
2. Poor people spend greater portion of their income than rich to narrow the consumption gap
3. current abs and relative income levels determine


### Demonstration effect

1. They have a tendency to imitate consumption habits of other people
2. Duessenberry effect

### Ratchet effect

1. High level of consumption is irreversible
2. Household will not reduce their consumption as their income falls
3. They become accustomed to their previous higher level of consumption
4. They will try to maintain their earlier consumption by reducing their savings


## Features of Relative Income Hypothesis

1. If relative income falls, APC rises
2. If relative income rises, APC falls
3. APC = MPC in the long run
