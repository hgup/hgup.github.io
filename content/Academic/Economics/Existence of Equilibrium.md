#  Equilibrium
## Existence of Equilibrium

![[Existence of Equilibrium 2024-10-01 09.15.43.excalidraw.light.svg]]

Let the demand function $D = D(P)$, supply function be $S=S(P)$;
- If they intersect in the first quadrant at one point, the equilibrium exists and it is unique. ($I$)
- If they intersect in any other quadrant then equilibrium doesn’t exist. ($II$ and $III$)
- If more than one point intersect, then equilibrium exists , but its not unique. ($IV$)

## Static Equilibrium[^1]

Suppose there is disturbance in the equilibrium either due to change in [[demand]] or change in [[supply]]. But the disturbance results in coming back to the original position, due to adjustment of market forces. Then the equilibrium is said to be **stable**, otherwise it is said to be **unstable**. This is called static stability.[^2]

If the stability is achieved through several periods of times in the long run, then it is said to be in **dynamic stability**. In dynamic stability, $P_t$[^3] $\to P_e$[^4] as $t \to \infty$.
![[Existence of Equilibrium 2024-10-01 09.31.34.excalidraw.light.svg]]

Excess demand function, $E(P) = D(P) - S(P)$ 

There are three possibilities
1. When $E(P) \gt 0$, when price is less than equilibrium price i.e. $P_0 \lt P_e$
2. When $E(P) \lt 0$, when price is more than equilibrium price i.e. $P_0 \gt P_e$
3. When $E(P) = 0$, when price is equal to equilibrium price i.e. $P_0 = P_e$

- Walrus assumes that if excess demand is positive, then there is a tendency on the part of the consumer to **rise the price** and if excess demand is negative, there is a tendency on the part of the seller to **reduce the price**.
- If Wal Russian assumption is working in the market, then any reduction in the price, below $P_e$ will make the consumer to rise it till equilibrium is reached.
- If it further rises, then seller will pull it down till equilibrium is reached.
- Thus there will be a mechanism in the market itself to see that equilibrium is maintained.
- If Wal Russian assumption is operating, excess demand is reduced either in the downward direction or in the upward direction.
- Thus $E(P)$ is a decreasing function of price. Thus $\dfrac{dE}{dP}\lt 0$, which implies that slope of demand function is less than slope of supply function $D’(P) \lt S’(P)$.
- Thus, according to Walrus, there is a static stability if slope of demand function is less that slope of supply function. This condition is **true for normal commodity**[^5]


[^1]: Equilibrium is the state of an object where it stays without motion in the absence of a disturbance
[^2]: Once we disturb the ball in a bowl, it will still achieve its equilibrium
[^3]: Price in time period $t$
[^4]: Equilibrium price
[^5]: Any exceptional thing will be covered in Marshallian demand functions.
